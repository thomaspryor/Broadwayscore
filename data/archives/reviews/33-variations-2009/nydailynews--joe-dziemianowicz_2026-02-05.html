Jane Fonda back on Broadway for '33 Variations'

Tuesday, March 10th 2009, 12:06 PM

Joan Marcus

Broadway’s "33 Variations."

There's a mystery humming in "33 Variations."

Why did Ludwig van Beethoven spend his final years feverishly rewriting a mediocre waltz by his publisher Anton Diabelli?

But the bigger question is what took Jane Fonda 46 years to come back to Broadway, where she's giving a robust and confident performance in the play that opened last night.

Fonda plays Katherine Brandt, a music scholar with a progressive, degenerative condition who's determined to understand Beethoven's fixation before her time is up.

The Oscar winner brings everything to this role that's made her an iconic film star: Pure enthusiasm, toughness tempered by vulnerability, and that distinctive voice which makes every line fascinating.

Too bad this handsomely designed but unconvincing drama isn't as big an event as Fonda's return. Writer-director Moises Kaufman ("Gross Indecency," "The Laramie Project") is known for visually inventive works that blend fact and fiction.

He delivers both in this play, seen earlier in San Diego and Washington, D.C. But what begins as an exciting journey of discovery, with hints of "Amadeus," sinks into a conventional Lifetime momand-daughter reconciliation.

And Katherine's conclusion about Beethoven is as sketchy as her character.

The action takes place in the past and present and tells three stories: The ailing Katherine's research in Bonn, Germany; Beethoven's stages of dementia and illness while writing the variations in the 1820s; and the growing romance between Katherine's nurse, Mike (Colin Hanks) and her daughter Clara (Samantha Mathis).

Two strands would have sufficed, since the third is contrived. Also distracting is Kaufman's tendency to hammer the paral lels between the two eras, including when characters speak lines in unison and Katherine is chided for treating Clara like "a second rate waltz." Nope, not subtle.

Despite the script's weaknesses, the cast is strong.

Zach Grenier's bluster and brooding are the right notes for the tempestuous genius and Mathis' quiet realism works wonderfully for Clara. As Gertie, a German archivist helping Katherine, Susan Kellermann employs light touches that take the role beyond stern stereotype.

Hanks, as Mike; Erik Steele, as Beethoven's assistant; and Don Amendolia, as Diabelli, are all engaging.

The stylish staging tickles the senses. Derek McLane's set, which features twirling panels covered in sheet music, and David Lander's beautiful lighting are first-rate. Adding another layer of interest is concert pianist Diane Walsh, who accompanies the action by playing Beethoven's variations.
