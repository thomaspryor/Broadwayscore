Send in the substitutes

  • By ELISABETH VINCENTELLI
  • Last Updated: 7:02 AM, August 2, 2010
  • Posted: 1:03 AM, August 2, 2010
Elisabeth Vincentelli
Blog: Theater

THEATER REVIEW

A LITTLE NIGHT MUSIC Walter Kerr Theatre, 219 W. 48th St.; 212-239-6200.

By stepping into the revival of Stephen Sondheim's "A Little Night Music," Berna dette Peters and Elaine Stritch are doing more than merely replacing Catherine Zeta-Jones and Angela Lansbury, respectively. They -- especially Peters -- have transformed the entire show. Trevor Nunn's production felt murky and undernourished when it opened back in December. Now, a coherent whole has emerged.

If Mrs. Michael Douglas won a Tony for her one-note performance as charismatic theater actress Desirée Armfeldt, you've got to wonder what Peters should get. A Medal of Honor? The Nobel Prize?

Joan Marcus
Bernadette Peters (with Alexander Hanson) gives Sondheim's musical the finesse and coherence it lacked.

Peters creates a multifaceted woman strong enough to anchor Sondheim's bittersweet comedy about romantic entanglements. She is the axle around which the show rotates, and her presence is felt even when she isn't onstage.

Of course, this Desirée remains the seductive, playful stage star she's meant to be. But she isn't young anymore, and is fully aware of the passage of time.

Being reunited with her old lover Fredrik Egerman (Alexander Hanson) prompts an avalanche of mixed emotions. It's a rare pleasure to watch them all flicker on Peters' face, to catch a sudden melancholy briefly cloud her gaze even as she unleashes her plentiful charms on her conquests.

And her "Send in the Clowns" is one for the books, a masterpiece of interpretative finesse that should be retitled "Send in the Handkerchiefs."

Lansbury was the best thing in the December cast, so the new Madame Armfeldt doesn't have the same radical impact as the new Desirée. Still, Stritch is a hoot to watch, as always. She spits out her bon mots with acidic relish and turns "Liaisons" into a mesmerizingly bizarre experience halfway between storytelling and performance art.

Like a rising tide lifting all boats, the newcomers have inspired the original cast to raise its game. Hanson, in particular, looks a lot more relaxed and isn't afraid to sound menacingly wolfish in "Now," while Erin Davie nails Charlotte's sarcastic disenchantment.

With everybody firing on all cylinders, Nunn's spare, twilit staging finally makes sense, and even the smallness of the orchestra feels appropriate. This is about presenting Sondheim's sorry-grateful, regretful-happy take on love, sex and marriage without pretenses or adornments -- but with plenty of style and wit. How adult!
