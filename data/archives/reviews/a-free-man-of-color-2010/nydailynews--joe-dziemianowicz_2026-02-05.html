A Free Man of Color

'A Free Man of Color' review: John Guare's new play is beautiful to look at, but unsatisfying

Joe Dziemianowicz

Friday, November 19th 2010, 4:00 AM

Courtesy of the Vivian Beaumont Theater
Lincoln Center's production of 'A Free Man of Color' is beautiful to look at, but unsatisfying.

Overview

  • Price: $65-$115
  • Reservations: Through Jan. 9
  • Phone: (212) 239-6200
  • Location: Vivian Beaumont Theatre, 150 W. 65th St.

Related News

  • The top 10 actors not to miss on and off-Broadway

You can't say John Guare's new play "A Free Man of Color" isn't ambitious in scope or awash in extravagant eye candy. Or that the huge cast of 33 isn't fully committed. But unfortunately that doesn't add up to a satisfying evening.

The production, which opened last night at Lincoln Center, suffers from a schizophrenic tone that jerks from light to dark and back again, cartoonlike characterizations and a confusing plot that skitters between the U.S., Spain, France and colonial Haiti. History buffs may have an edge.

A mix of allegory, Restoration comedy and social commentary, the story is seen through the eyes of the fictional Jacques Cornet (Jeffrey Wright). He's a rich, multiracial hedonist who's wired his life so he has everything he wants — access to high fashion, high living and high times with other men's wives. He also has a slave, Murmur (a fine Mos, formerly known as Mos Def) to serve him.

The setting is New Orleans at the start of the 19th century, where all varieties in sexuality and skin color are cause for celebration. That changes after the territory became part of the United States in 1803 in the Louisiana Purchase.

There, in a nutshell, is the play's point — all men are not created equal. It comes to light in the homestretch of the 2½-hour production in a conversation between Cornet and President Thomas Jefferson (John McMartin), who is assisted by Meriwether Lewis (Paul Dano). It feels like it takes a lot of play to communicate the obvious message.

Compounding the frustration is the production's exaggerated acting style and frantic little vignettes. At times, there's so much motion the play gets eaten up by all the machinery. The dialogue can even sound like jibberish.

Despite Wright's interesting performance, Cornet is an odd character to tell the story of the difficult march to racism. He's endowed with a sense of only looking out for himself.

George C. Wolfe directs with customary flair and imagination and has assembled a design team that fills the stage with stunning visuals. Ann Hould-Ward's costumes are posh and witty. David Rockwell's sets are beautiful. Jules Fisher and Peggy Eisenhouer's lighting adds drama. One wonders, though, if all the bells and whistles actually enhance the story.

You can see the money on the stage at the Beaumont, sure.

Too bad it's in a production that's got more style than substance.
