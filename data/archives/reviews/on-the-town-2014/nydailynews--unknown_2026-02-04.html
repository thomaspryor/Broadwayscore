‘On the Town’: Theater review 

By Joe Dziemianowicz
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
•
Oct 17, 2014 at 9:00 pm

Three footloose sailors aren't the only ones who get lucky in "On the Town."

The audience does, too.

Advertisement

Director John Rando has assembled a great cast for this fizzy and frisky revival of the 1944 musical by Betty Comden, Adolph Green and Leonard Bernstein. Tracing a tale of World War II tars on leave in the big city, the production feels like a big, juicy kiss. So pucker up, New York.

The 28-piece orchestra brings out all the splendor in Bernstein's rapturous, red-blooded score. Musical gems sparkle, starting with the rousing "New York, New York" ("it's a helluva town"), which gets things going. The playful "I Can Cook Too" and wistful "Some Other Time" follow in short order.

Advertisement

Comden and Green's story unfolds as lighthearted episodes. The Navy men set out to see the sights — architectural and anatomical.

Romantic Gabey (Tony Yazbeck) dashes in hot pursuit of dream girl Ivy (Megan Fairchild), who he spies on a poster.

Meanwhile, naive Chip (Jay Armstrong Johnson) gets hijacked by randy cabbie Hildy (Alysha Umphress). And goofy seaman Ozzie (Clyde Alves) and hot-to-trot anthropologist Claire (Elizabeth Stanley) sing about getting "Carried Away" and do just that. They jump each other's bones near a dinosaur skeleton at the Museum of Natural History.

Rando ("Urinetown") mines the script for all its boisterous humor and smartly makes space for hushed interludes, including a touching version of the melancholy "Lonely Town." That one spills into the audience.

The show is based on a ballet by Jerome Robbins. Choreographer Joshua Bergasse keeps the fleet of agile and athletic dancers leaping, kicking and spinning like a top.

The cast is top-to-bottom excellent. In sailor suits and stripped to their skivvies, Johnson and Alves are adorable and able-bodied. Umphress adds sass and brass as the go-for-it taxi driver, and Stanley is delicious as the oversexed scientist. Jackie Hoffman is a hoot-and-a-half playing a boozy voice teacher and a few more small roles.

Yazbeck is a very appealing and persuasive song-and-dance man. His pas pas de deux with Fairchild, dancing dynamite from New York City Ballet, is poignant and sexy.

The look of the show is chipper and bright, including Jess Goldstein's perky costumes and Jason Lyons' lighting. Beowulf Boritt's sets, though, are head-scratchers. Set pieces add modern flourishes but overdo the cartoonishness. That includes clear plastic skyscrapers and a lemon-yellow T. rex.

Even so, it's a helluva entertainment.
