NY1 Theater Review: "Born Yesterday"

By: Roma Torre

Garson Kanin's 1946 play "Born Yesterday" is back on Broadway. NY1's Roma Torre filed the following report.

"Born Yesterday" is 65 years old, and while it’s very much a product of its time, the current production proves it stands the test of time. Garson Kanin’s play, intended as an indictment of corruption and the abuse of political power, certainly resonates today.

But any production of the populist drama largely depends on the strength of its two central performances, and on that front, the latest staging is likely to gain a whole new generation of fans.

Judy Holliday created the role of Billie Dawn on stage and film and set a benchmark that’s been impossible to top ever since. As a floozie plucked from the chorus line by the rich thug Harry Brock, she is endearingly clueless. Kanin, playing off the Pygmalion theme, transforms this dim bulb into an enlightened citizen.

It’s an acting challenge that’s sunk many a decent actress, but rising star Nina Arianda nailed it. With a bow to Holliday’s sweet naiveté, Arianda makes the role her own. She gets a huge assist from costume designer Catherine Zuber, who drapes her fabulously svelte figure in elegantly ostentatious fashion.

As her foil, Jim Belushi is a perfect fit, seemingly born to play this larger-than-life brute. He’s a natural, bellowing Harry’s vulgarisms, but beneath the menace, Belushi shades the performance enough to reveal a spark of humanity.

Both lead actors, along with Robert Sean Leonard in the pivotal role of Billie's tutor Paul Verrall, manage to delve beyond stereotype.

Doug Hughes' staging is first-rate, finding just the right balance between the humor and Kanin's serious message.

Kanin's idealism may seem preachy, but given the state of the union today, our democratic principles can certainly bear repeating. No matter how familiar you are with the work, this fine production offers plenty of compelling reasons to return to "Born Yesterday."
