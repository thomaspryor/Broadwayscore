Review: Storytelling Reinvented in “Sea Wall / A Life”

Christian Lewis
4 min read
·
Aug 9, 2019

Broadway is no stranger to the limited engagement, one-man show. I could say “one-person,” but they have almost always been men, sadly (with the notable exception of the autobiographical and extraordinary work of Heidi Shrek in “What the Constitution Means to Me). On the surface, the double bill of “Sea Wall” by Simon Stephens and “A Life” by Nick Payne playing at the Hudson Theatre can seem quite similar to another recent piece, “Stories By Heart” (John Lithgow) — but only on the surface. Although these works are all performed by a single man narrating a story, “Sea Wall / A Life” is a critical departure from the more traditional, one-man shows that Broadway has become used to.

On the basic level, it is different because this time we get two: two short plays, two different actors, two stories. Together they make a fascinating pairing that is equally captivating and devastating. Although they may not share any direct overlap, both stories explore themes of family, love, fatherhood, death, loss, and grief. They are Greek tragedies for the modern age, full of pathos and heart-wrenching prose.
