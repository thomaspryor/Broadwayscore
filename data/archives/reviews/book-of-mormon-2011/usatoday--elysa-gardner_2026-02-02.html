Review: 'Book of Mormon' is gloriously juvenile, surprisingly sincere

Kerry Lengel
The Republic | azcentral.com
Updated Oct. 22, 2015, 6:11 p.m. ET
Keep Watching


Packed with F-bombs, blatant blasphemy and enough potty humor to make a 13-year-old boy hyperventilate, Broadways The Book of Mormon is every bit as outrageous as its reputation.

Underneath all that, however, it is also surprisingly sweet, and it's even one of the most traditional musicals to hit it big in recent memory.

It is, in short, a five-star knockout, and it has finally come to Tempes ASU Gammage, three years after the national tour launched and after pretty much every other market in the country has seen it (sometimes twice). Heck, the show even went first to Salt Lake City, home base of the religion that it mercilessly and lovingly lampoons.

'Mormon' star Alexandra Ncube is from Tempe

The 2011 Tony Award winner for best musical, The Book of Mormon was created by Trey Parker and Matt Stone, the dirty minds behind TVs South Park, along with Avenue Q and Frozen songwriter Robert Lopez. It tells the story of two young missionaries sent from Utah to Uganda, only to find that the locals are too worried about poverty, violence and disease to listen to teenage boys preaching about Another Testament of Jesus Christ.

The putative hero of the story is Elder Price, a gung-ho alpha Mormon whos certain hes destined for greatness. His assigned companion, Elder Cunningham, on the other hand, is a needy nerd who knows more about Star Wars and The Lord of the Rings than the gospel he is preaching. (So the Bible is actually a trilogy, and the Book of Mormon is Return of the Jedi, he enthuses during All American Prophet.)

On Broadway, these roles were originated, respectively, by Andrew Rannells and Josh Gad, both of whom have moved on to more star turns on stage and screen. Yet their current stand-ins on the first national tour, Billy Harrigan Tighe and A.J. Holmes, dont merely do justice to the characters, they make them their own.

Tighe preens hilariously in his dance numbers and displays a soaring tenor on numbers such as I Believe. And Holmes, looking a bit like Jimmy Fallons awkward kid brother, has mastered the art of playing a lovable loser. (He sings less but has pipes to match his co-star as well.)

The excellent cast also features Arizona State University alumna Alexandra Ncube as the leading lady, a sweetly naive villager named Nabulungi. She is as winsomely sincere singing a wistful ode to the paradise known as Sal Tlay Ka Siti as you could ask, earning much-deserved hometown cheers from the audience.

Despite the R-rated content, Book of Mormon is not as unlikely a hit as might first seem to be the case. Parker and Stone had plenty of practice parodying show tunes on South Park, and here they rise above mere parody. Yes, there are jokey nods to The Lion King and Annie, but this shows eminently hummable tunes stand on their own, especially the first-act showstopper Turn It Off, which features a tap-dance break worthy of 42nd Street.

The production values are great, but this show doesnt rely on elaborate spectacle to keep eyeballs glued to the stage. Instead, it uses expert storytelling, deftly doling out information, particularly about the Mormon faith, that the audience needs to understand the jokes. (No, not every detail is accurate, but if you dont know anything about church doctrine, this musical definitely qualifies as a crash course.)

New York Times critic Ben Brantley declared The Book of Mormon to be the best musical of the century. That, of course, was when the century was only 10 years old. Now, it already has competition from acclaimed hits such as Hamilton. But even if it doesnt hold its title as the best musical in a hundred years, it will probably be remembered as the funniest.

The laughs roll nonstop, from the manic fantasia of Spooky Mormon Hell Dream to the Up With People-esque We Are Africa, sung, of course, by a bevy of very White boys. Yet there is also a heart-felt message to this story, which is that even if religion any religion doesnt always stand up to logical scrutiny, it can still be a force for good in peoples lives, and in the world.

There are many, of course, who will find this kind of irreverence and shock value to be disrespectful and even hateful. But for those who sympathize with the shows unexpectedly tender humanism, The Book of Mormon just may be, well, a religious experience.
