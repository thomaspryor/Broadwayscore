The Book of Mormon: a sorry disappointment

2011/03/24 16:06:00
  • Email
  • Print
  • Rss
  • Article
  • Photos (2)

Rema Webb, Andrew Rannells, and Josh Gad in The Book of Mormon.

By Richard Ouzounian Theatre Critic

NEW YORK When the most outrageous comedic minds writing today decide to unleash their energies in creating an irreverent Broadway musical to end all irreverent Broadway musicals, its very disappointing to have to ask one question.

Why isnt it funnier?

And, perhaps even more to the point, why isnt it more outrageous?

The Book of Mormon, which opened on Thursday night at the Eugene ONeill Theatre, is the long-awaited creation of Trey Parker and Matt Stone, best known as the brains behind South Park and Robert Lopez, one of the creators of Avenue Q.

With parents like that, you expect something that would cause shivers of outrage to run like seismic shocks throughout the cultural landscape.

But instead, we get a good-natured, jocular, look at religion that with only a few exceptions wouldnt cause much indignation anywhere.

A great deal of attention was devoted to the one of the shows opening numbers, in which a duo of misguided Mormon missionaries in Africa learn to sing a scatological song about the great omniscient deity.

Yes, that number occurs and its pretty darn funny and certainly outrageous, but the truly distressing news is that its all downhill from there, both in terms of scandal and comedy.

Parker, Stone and Lopez seem to be so giddily self-congratualtory over the fact that theyre mocking religion in a musical that they seem to have missed the fact that their reputations were built on saying the unsayable and doing the undoable.

There is more edgy satire in any of the episodes of South Park about the Catholic Church or the Church of Scientology than youll find on stage in The Book of Mormon.

The kind of head-shaking Did they really say that? reaction that any good episode of their TV series provokes is sadly lacking.

Whats left is a lot of Grade B, vaguely amateurish musical comedy antics, the kind of thing that director Casey Nicholaw is now making his stock in trade.

It worked in Spamalot, where the writing actually had more snap and verve and genuine outrage behind it. But here, it all starts to look like a campy collegiate revue making fun of black culture.

The Book of Mormon treats political revolution, female circumcision and other serious issues as the topics for cheap one-liners, neither offering searing social commentary nor boffo laughter.

The cast all work hard, but one starts to wonder what for. Josh Gad plays the Seth Rogen Mormon, while Andrew Rannells is the Bradley Cooper one. Rannells earns points for his unswerving intensity and clean-cut charm, but its all to little avail.

The bottom line is that the show we all hoped would set Broadway on its ear, simply pats it on its back, providing us with generic pastiche comedy and timidly tepid satire.

O tempora, o mores! O Parker, o Stone! We expected so much more,
