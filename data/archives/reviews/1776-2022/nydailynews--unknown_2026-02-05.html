Theater review: ‘1776’ concert staging is witty and well-timed, but it's sometimes long-winded 

By Joe Dziemianowicz
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
Mar 31, 2016 at 12:06 pm

Freedom doesn't come easy-breezy.

Either does "1776," a singing slice of Americana about the Declaration of Independence.

Advertisement

Back in an Encores! concert staging through Sunday, the 1969 best musical Tony winner by Peter Stone (book) and Sherman Edwards (music and lyrics) packs wit, spirit and some good songs.

"For God's Sake, John, Sit Down" establishes a snarky, non-reverential tone from the get-go. "He Plays the Violin" lends romance. "Momma, Look Sharp," stings and pulls heart-strings.

Advertisement

But long-winded scenes without music — or so much as a drumbeat or toot on a fife — are as enjoyable as taxation without representation. Stretches of the almost three-hour show are a slog.

Still, Stone's witty jokes about a do-nothing Congress never fail to hit the mark. Polarizing founding father John Adams (a charismatic Santino Fontana) does gags that could work for a Jimmy Fallon monologue.

Director Garry Hynes' production, which uses color-blind casting, features modern clothes for Benjamin Franklin (a droll John Larroquette) and Thomas Jefferson (an appealing John Behlmann) and the other statesmen and a couple of wives.

These 18th-century figures are deciding whether or not to declare independence and fight for freedom from England, but they could be going to a board meeting tomorrow.

Set over three tempestuous and bloody months running up to July, 4, 1776, the show's return is perfectly timed for current, high-stakes politics.
