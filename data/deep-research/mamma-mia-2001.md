## Detailed Financial Analysis (Feb 2026)

### Capitalization

The original Broadway production of Mamma Mia! at the Winter Garden Theatre had an initial capitalization of about $10 million. Playbill confirmed that Mamma Mia! "has recouped its entire $10 million Broadway investment." Universal Music (ABBA's record label) contributed roughly half of this $10 million investment -- an unusual arrangement reflecting the music rights-holder's strong backing.

### Recoupment

Mamma Mia! recouped its initial $10 million investment in "less than 28 weeks" of performances (Playbill, late May 2002). The show opened October 18, 2001, so recoupment came by spring 2002 -- one of the fastest returns on investment in Broadway history.

### Weekly Running Costs

Contemporaneous reports from the early 2000s suggest that Mamma Mia! operated with a weekly running cost in the mid six-figures. A 2005 Broadway column noted that a comparably large musical had a weekly running cost around $600,000. Mamma Mia! likely had a similar weekly operating expense ($500-600K per week in the early years). Earning around $800K-$1M in gross weekly ticket sales at full capacity, the production cleared a few hundred-thousand in profit each week, enabling swift recoupment.

### SVOG Grant

N/A. The original Broadway production ended its run on September 12, 2015, so by the time the pandemic shutdown hit in 2020, Mamma Mia! was no longer running on Broadway. No SVOG grant was awarded.

### Investor Structure and ABBA's Involvement

One notable aspect of Mamma Mia!'s production is the direct involvement of ABBA's songwriters as producers/investors -- an unusual arrangement:

- **Bjorn Ulvaeus** is officially credited as a lead producer (with Judy Craymer and Richard East under the Littlestar banner, in association with Universal Music)
- **Benny Andersson** invested in the show alongside Ulvaeus
- **Anni-Frid Lyngstad** was also an investor -- "Ulvaeus, Benny Andersson, and later Lyngstad were all investors in the original Mamma Mia!" (Fortune, Sept 2021)
- **Universal Music** (which owns ABBA's catalog) contributed ~50% of the $10M investment and was a partner

**Dual income structure:** As composers/lyricists, Andersson and Ulvaeus earned royalties on every performance (a percentage of gross box office). As lead producers/investors, they also received a share of net profits. Littlestar's financial reports show that in one recent year, the company paid out £5.7 million in fees to the principals, which "included income from productions and music royalties," alongside a £900,000 dividend to their owner companies (The Guardian, Jan 2024).

**Investor implications:** A chunk of weekly revenue went to the creators as authors' royalties (off-the-top, standard industry practice), but since Andersson and Ulvaeus were also stakeholders, those payments went to the show's own producers rather than outside parties. This unique arrangement ensured the songwriters had creative control and significant upside, while aligning their interests with investors.
